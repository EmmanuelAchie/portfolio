01
The Problem

Why pickup games fail

Neighborhood pickup basketball is one of the most accessible forms of recreational sport — no league sign-up, no fees, no schedule. But anyone who's played regularly knows the frustrations: you show up to an empty court, or you show up and the skill gap is so wide it stops being fun, or you text six people only to get two confirmed responses.

The core problem is a coordination failure. There's no shared system. People rely on informal networks — group chats, word of mouth, just showing up and hoping — and it breaks down constantly. The result is wasted trips, low turnout, and games that exclude new players who don't already know the regulars.

The design challenge: build something that reduces friction for casual players while also respecting the organic, spontaneous nature of pickup basketball. This isn't a league management app — it needed to feel as casual as the game itself.

Problem Statement: How can we better support coordination, inclusion, and reliability in neighborhood pickup basketball communities without disrupting their informal and spontaneous nature?

§ 02 — Research & Pain Points

What the Research Revealed

📅
Inconsistent Attendance
Players commit informally and back out with no notice. Organizers can't reliably predict how many people will show up, making it impossible to plan a proper game.
⚖️
No Skill-Based Matching
Games often collapse when skill levels are wildly mismatched — experienced players dominate, new players disengage, and nobody has a good time. There's no mechanism to filter by level. These issues disproportionately affect new or less confident players, who may feel intimidated or excluded by unclear norms and a lack of transparency.
🔒
Closed Communities
Pickup games rely on existing social networks. New residents or visitors have no way to find games, and regulars have no easy way to recruit fresh players when turnout is low.
§ 02b — Core Features Designed

5+ Features Designed from Research

01
Nearby Game Discovery
A scroll picker and map-based view of active games within a user-defined radius, showing court, time, skill level, and current player count at a glance.
02
Filtering
Games are tagged by skill level (beginner / intermediate / competitive). Players filter to find games that match their ability. Players can also filter for location, game availability, and times.
03
Game Creation Flow
A minimal creation flow lets anyone post a game in under a minute — court, time, skill level, and max players. No account required to browse.
04
Real-Time Check In
Confirmed attendance updates live so organizers know who's actually coming, not just who said they might show up.
05
Post-Game Feedback
Five star rating after each game improves future matching, helps identify active courts, and builds community trust over time.

User Personas

Who I designed for

I developed personas from research into the distinct types of users pickup basketball communities attract, thus, not just the regulars, but the people who want to participate but can't find a way in.

Wesley, 32
The Regular Organizer

"I'm always the one texting everyone. Half the time people bail last minute and I can't get a full 5-on-5 together. “I want to help my community grow stronger together but it is difficult sometimes"

Needs: Reliable RSVP system, real-time headcount, easy way to recruit players

Damien, 20
The Newcomer

"I just moved here for college and I want to play but I don't know anyone and I don't know where people play. I am Nervous but excited to try this new experience."

Needs: Game discovery without social connections, skill-level visibility, low-stakes entry

Aisha, 30
The Casual Player

"I'm still learning, but playing basketball helps clear my mind as life after university can be unsettling."

Needs: Skill-level filtering, competitive game tags, consistent regulars

Nicole, 41
No Experience

"I am not interested in sports. I love making crafts and reading books."

Needs: N/A

04
My Process

End-to-end UX design

1

Competitive Analysis

I audited existing apps, such as PickupPal, InfinteHoops, GoodRec, and Endalgo, to understand what existed and where the gaps were. Most were either too formal (league tools & pay-to-play) or too general (event apps) to serve the casual pickup use case well.

2

Persona Development & Story Mapping

I built four distinct personas (see above) and ran story mapping sessions to trace each user's full journey — from Activity 1: realizing they want to play and discovering games, Activity 8 to Ratings & Reviews. This surfaced where each persona's journey broke down and what features were non-negotiable for each.

3

Low-Fidelity Wireframes (Paper)

I started on paper, using low-tech which allowed me to move fast without getting attached to visual choices. I sketched 10 different screens while mainly focsuing on the 4 core screens (map view, game detail, check-in, creation flow) before evaluating which layouts best served each persona's needs.

4

Digital Wireframes & High-Fidelity Prototype

I moved into Figma for digital wireframes, then refined into a full high-fidelity prototype with real components, color, and interaction states. The final prototype covers the complete user journey across all 6 flow stages — fully interactive and clickable.

05
Takeaways

What I learned

  • Starting with research before touching any digital tools was the most important decision I made. Every design choice I made in high-fidelity was traceable back to a real user need or pain point from the research phase.
  • Personas are most useful when they have genuine tension between them. Designing for Wesley (the organizer) and Damien (the newcomer) forced me to make real trade-offs rather than defaulting to one type of user.
  • Paper wireframes are extremely benefitial. The prototype emphasizes key user tasks, and successfully explored key interactions and highlighted areas of uncertainty, especially regarding coordination and real-time feedback. Moving fast on paper meant I could throw away multiple layout ideas in the time it would have taken me to build one in Figma.
  • The hardest design problem wasn't the UI — it was balancing casual accessibility with enough structure to solve the coordination problem. However, doing so without taking too much away from the authentic experience of pickup basketball. Too much friction and people won't bother. Too little and the app can't solve the attendance problem it was built to fix.
